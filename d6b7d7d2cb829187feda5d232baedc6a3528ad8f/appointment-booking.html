CommBank Digital Appointment Booking 

Digital Mobilisation - Entire Lender Workforce

Multi-year program to systemise calendar schedules and profiles of Australia’s largest frontline lending workforce

Background

CommBank holds the largest share of home loans in Australia. It’s reputation to service and maintain customer satisfaction is of the highest priority and the fact is, customers choose CommBank because of the flexibility to transact digitally and seamlessly.

In 2016, the bank embarked on a multi-year program with the goal of systemising the profiles and calendars of thousands of lenders. As a result, the bank has up to the minute visibility of the schedules of our entire frontline workforce.

The final strategic maneuverer was to replace a decade old manual process of back and forth calling to schedule appointments with home lending specialists. Finally in 2018, we released ‘Digital Appointment Booking’ nationally to all current and prospective customers.

Discovery: contextual analysis and interviews

Rapid prototyping and testing

We began to put together a skeleton of the digital experience we wanted to offer and began testing, collecting data and iterating our design.

Research themes

i. Pre-filtering results

We tried: To give users the ability to filter by appointment channel, times and languages from a single centralised screen (screen 1 below)

We learnt: This was a friction point for almost all users, the mental capacity needed to understand what we were asking defeated the whole purpose of presenting these options. Unlike the CBA app where it’s used daily/weekly. This app is used once every few months/years - it was not ideal to establish this complex single use interaction.

Solution: Our established typeform pattern is used to collect loan details, so we reused the same approach to set appointment filters. By breaking the single centralised screen into 2 discrete questions, the friction point experienced in the initial design was eradicated.

ii. Loaded actions

We know: CommBank customers are often time poor and love the flexibility to conduct their business online or via voice call.

We learnt: By carefully pre-empting the next most likely action, we could triage customers to the most relevant destination.

Also learnt: Placing the triage point early in the process meant customers that prefer a phone call did not need to choose a location saving time.

iii. Connecting with faces

We tried: People see faces and the connection is instant - this is human nature. As a part of our lender profiles, we have corporate profile-images of all our front-line staff members and this became a major theme in all our designs. Naturally, we used these images as a part of our initial prototypes.

We learnt: Users responded exceptionally to profile images as we had expected. But we began to notice, users were overwhelmingly sorting appointment results by the lender profile-image which was a consequence of selective customer biases instead of finding the most appropriate available time.

iv. Scalable availability pattern

We learnt: Users were able to build mental model of our calendar pattern very quickly. On any given day, we would only show no more than 9 options per day (as seen above) while also providing an indication of the capacity of each appointment channel with (+ n more).

We also learnt: By carefully managing the amount of results/options presented, it allowed users to freely browse without being mentally overwhelmed and/or needing to scroll excessively to find a suitable appointment.

v. User interface polish

I was responsible for pixel perfect interface polish, content writing and working with developers to fine tune the CSS across all viewports.

vi. Negative scenarios

I worked with developers and business analysts to capture negative scenarios like the days we have limited / no availability, finding other branches that may have availability and many other scenarios.
