NAB.design - Personal Everyday Banking (Unsecured Lending)

Global NAB Expense Capture Pattern

  • Learning 1: Flexibility & Utility
    Provide 2 optimised routes and provide users the flexibility to move between them

    Persona 1: Financially savvy (40%) Manages expenses in a spreadsheet / app. They can distill all their expenses into a single sum.

    Persona 2: Needs a little help (60%) They can't proceed with certainty without some help.

  • Learning 2: Information Density
    Customers valued having category inputs within proximity to aid tweaking and minor fixes before submission

    The single page approach: Means the design becomes more robust, and the increased manoeuvrability of the interaction makes it easier for customers to see the relationships between categories and make changes along the way.

    Sub-page approach: Increases friction but focusses the task and reduces overall visual noise.

  • Learning 3: Information Architecture
    We assumed customers understood how NAB/APRA has defined expense capture

    A card sort exercise with customers quickly revealed that an expense can fit into more than 1 category.

    Sequencing categories effectively increased input accuracy: e.g. Car Insurance can fit into 'Vehicle & Transport' or 'Insurance'. Surfacing 'Insurance' section first positively impacted data capture accuracy.

    Grouping categories effectively decreased confusion and rework: e.g.'Housing Expenses' and 'Investment property' have shared commonalities, but were placed a great distance away from each other which resulted in input rework.

  • Learning 4: Save
    A sad experience is when task completion is propelled by sunk cost fallacy

    Fatigued Users: Remove any anxiety that navigating away is destructive.

    Design Intervention / Business Value (ratio): A trivial intervention provides manueverability in the last mile of the credit card journey.

  • Learning 5: Tuning Language
    Content strategy: more specific vs. less specific

    Hypothesis: Suggest expense types like 'Netflix' and hope that starts a train of thought to think about related expenses like 'e.g. DisneyPlus'.

    Reality: This negatively narrowed the scope of what customers thought we were asking and many took the instructions as literal.

    Design control: This informed our content strategy and how we provide examples for what's expected in each category.

  • Learning 6: Content Precision
    Be specific and leave less for interpretation where possible

    Incorrect input errors are directly correlated to negative credit outcomes

    Do I provide my mortgage?: Significant expense for most, and an equal portion of customers would add their repayments in 'Housing expenses'.

    Are asking about an investment or residential asset?: The savvy has a clear distinction between principle place of residence (PPOR) or an investment expenses.

    Impact: This would skew customer's financial profile, resulting in a decline for many applications and a material impact on conversion.

  • Learning 7: Feedback & Progression
    Lengthy interactions like this need to be providing users with feedback periodically

    • Learning 8: Effective Triage
      Most customers can't distill their expenses into a single figure with accuracy

      Emphasis and visual affordance: We present the optimal route and optimise the design so customers can find help if they need it.

      Learning 9: Layout to Educate
      Optimal layout to help customers think about their expenses

      Video eye tracking: We tracked the user's eye movement upon seeing the design for the first time and noticed users using the left column to complete the right column.
